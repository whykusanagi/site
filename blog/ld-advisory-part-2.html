The Short Version

The operator behind the LDPlayer Android emulator, the LD Cloud streaming service, and the LDShop account marketplace was leaving twenty-three publicly listable cloud storage buckets exposed. Inside those buckets were database backups, admin bastion-host dumps, and several hundred video recordings of users entering their credentials into games on LD's cloud product.

Twelve days have passed. The buckets are closed. Most of the parties I notified are silent. This post is about what's inside the software the operator has been shipping unchanged the entire time — and about the operator's twelve days of public silence.

How the Disclosure Went Down

The bucket list originated on a Telegram channel called mainleakflow. @NTE_WL reposted it to X on May 13 in a thread with screenshots:

x.com/NTE_WL/status/2054655640434348255 ↗

That post is where I first saw it.

Another X user, @nozomi_clips, publicly pressed @NTE_WL for direct proof that the screenshots actually linked to LD. It was a reasonable challenge. An email list and a nine-minute video do not, on their own, prove a connection.

@NTE_WL replied at 1:10 PM on May 13 with two pre-signed Huawei OBS URLs pointing at specific files inside LD's buckets. That reply hit 68.4K views before the signed URLs expired roughly six hours later.

The Signed URLs Deserve a Beat

A public-listing misconfiguration is one kind of problem. Pre-signed OBS URLs are a different kind, because they require both the cloud account's access key ID and its matching secret to generate.

So whoever produced those URLs had more than visibility into publicly listable buckets. They had full LD-account cloud credentials.

That points to one of two things:

  1. A credential compromise. Keys leaked through a code repository, another exposed system, or insider exfiltration.
  2. Insider access to LD's cloud account at the source.

Either reading is more severe than "they left a bucket public." Either reading should have triggered an internal incident response on LD's side, independently of the public-listing problem.

// Live IOC

The access key ID embedded in those signed URLs is now a public indicator of compromise. Masked here as O5UJ...CQPD. The full value is in @NTE_WL's post for anyone who needs it. Until LD rotates that key, anyone with the matching secret can keep producing signed URLs against any bucket on the same account. The Huawei lockdown only blocks anonymous listing. It does not invalidate credentialed access.

What Happened Next

I verified the contents independently and posted Part 1 the same evening. Disclosure emails went to Huawei Cloud abuse, Kuro Games, HoYoverse, and Yostar, plus a separate Have I Been Pwned submission.

@NTE_WL later confirmed publicly that the buckets had been locked down:

x.com/NTE_WL/status/2054940524369379498 ↗

Timestamped 8:02 AM, May 14. That puts the bucket lockdown at roughly nineteen hours after @NTE_WL's public verification proof, and a similar window after Part 1 went out. Fast turnaround. Almost certainly Huawei Cloud's abuse team acting on the disclosure email I sent the same evening.

The Question Part 1 Didn't Answer

Part 1 was about LD Cloud. That is the streaming product, the one where the Android instance runs on LD's servers and you receive a video stream back. LD Cloud has a relatively small user base.

The product almost everyone in the gacha community uses is the local LDPlayer emulator. The one you download to your PC and run yourself. The user base there is tens of millions.

So: when you install LDPlayer locally on your machine, what does it phone home about? Is that telemetry going to the same operator whose cloud buckets just leaked?

To answer it, I looked inside the actual Android image that LDPlayer ships. Three different versions, captured eight months apart:

  • The offline installer LDPlayer's CDN has been serving since September 2025
  • A clean Windows install from earlier this year
  • LDPlayer 9.5.11.1, the current public release, dated May 6

That last one matters on its own. It dropped seven days before the bucket disclosure, and it is still the most recent thing LDPlayer has shipped publicly. As of writing, twenty days later, no new version has appeared.

A Correction From Part 1

I want to flag something I got wrong before going further.

In Part 1 I hypothesised that LDPlayer ships with Sogou Pinyin pre-installed. Sogou is the Chinese keyboard app with a long, documented telemetry history. If it had been the default keyboard inside LDPlayer, every keystroke any user typed into any app, including game password fields, would have hit Sogou first. That was the IME-keylogger theory in Part 1.

It turns out that is not what LDPlayer ships.

The default Chinese input method inside the LDPlayer Android image is Google's open-source PinyinIME, not Sogou. I confirmed this in all three system images.

If you never went and installed Sogou yourself through LDAppStore, that specific path is not active for you. Users who did install Sogou are still in scope for the IME-keylogger concern. Users who never touched it are not.

That correction narrows the affected group on one axis. The replacement findings are bigger.

What Is Actually Inside LDPlayer

When you install LDPlayer, the Android image laid down on your machine comes with a system app called LDAppStore. This is LD's own app marketplace.

The important detail is the privilege tier it runs at.

Android groups apps into tiers. Normal apps you install from Google Play run in a sandbox with limited permissions. System apps that ship inside the OS image run with much broader access. LDAppStore is in that second tier. It can install other apps silently, read device identifiers ordinary apps cannot reach, and self-grant permissions without prompting you.

Bundled inside LDAppStore is a third-party Chinese analytics SDK called MobTech.

// The Main Finding

LDAppStore — a system-tier app shipping in every LDPlayer install — bundles MobTech, an analytics SDK with documented broken encryption and unauthenticated TLS. The pathway is active for every user with LDPlayer installed, including users who never touched Sogou.

The Citizen Lab Corroboration

MobTech is not an unknown quantity.

In February 2025, the Citizen Lab research group at the University of Toronto published a detailed analysis of MobTech as part of their writeup of network security issues in the RedNote app (Xiaohongshu):

citizenlab.ca/2025/02/network-security-issues-in-rednote/ ↗

They documented exactly what data MobTech collects, how it transmits it, and the security properties of that transmission. Their findings:

  • The SDK sends HTTP POST requests to hxxps://devc[.]zztfly[.]com/dinfo
  • The payload contains your device's MAC address, available storage, RAM, screen size, and device model
  • The payload is encrypted with AES

Which sounds fine, until you read the next sentence.

The encryption key is hardcoded into the SDK as the bytestring:

sdk.commonap.sdk

That is not a typo.

The same encryption key is in every copy of the SDK that ships in any app. Anyone with a copy of the SDK can decrypt anyone else's payloads.

In the RedNote builds Citizen Lab analysed, TLS was used but without certificate validation. Anyone sitting on the network path could intercept or substitute the traffic.

The Same SDK Is In LDAppStore

Same endpoint cluster. Same encryption pattern. Same broken design.

If you have LDPlayer installed on your machine, your device fingerprint is being shipped to a Chinese analytics collector under encryption that is technically present but functionally absent.

That is the main finding. The rest is context.

The Rest of the Surveillance Surface

The same LDAppStore also ships with:

  • Tencent's Bugly crash-reporting library
  • Sentry, a second crash reporter
  • Hardcoded URLs for two telemetry collectors: middledata[.]ldmnq[.]com and middledata[.]ldplayer[.]net
  • Aliyun cloud storage buckets in Hangzhou and Singapore
  • Hardcoded URLs to LD's pre-production and test environments, left in production code (test endpoints typically have weaker authentication than production)

The most recent release, 9.5.11.1, adds:

  • A global Tencent Cloud instant messaging SDK with login endpoints across eight regions
  • Google AdMob ad serving

Individually, none of these is unusual for a Chinese consumer app. Together, on a privileged system app shipping to tens of millions of installs, the picture is: a wide and increasing telemetry surface, an operator who has demonstrated they will leave sensitive infrastructure publicly listable, and a third-party SDK with documented broken encryption.

And One More Thing: An Unpatched Operating System

The bundled Android version is 9 (Pie), released in 2018.

The security patch level inside the image is dated 2019-07-05.

That is almost seven years of accumulated Android security fixes that the LDPlayer base image has not received. Every monthly Android security bulletin since July 2019 applies to anything running inside the LDPlayer VM.

That is independent of the telemetry question. It is just an unpatched operating system that has been unpatched for a long time.

What I Am Not Claiming

I did not intercept MobTech traffic to verify what gets sent. The description above rests on Citizen Lab's analysis, which I am citing rather than reproducing.

I am not claiming intent on LD's part. The either-or framing from Part 1 still applies: either deliberate harvesting infrastructure that someone forgot to lock down, or a catastrophic misconfiguration on top of an otherwise legitimate operations platform. The new findings here do not push that question one way or the other. They show the scale and surface of the data the operator handles, regardless of intent.

Twelve Days Later: Who Responded

LD (Operator) SILENT

No statement on the operator's X account. No incident notice on their blog at hxxps://www[.]ldshop[.]gg/tw/blog. Only operator-adjacent response: messages from a moderator in LDShop's public Discord claiming they were "hacked by a competitor," no evidence offered.

No explanation of why user session recordings, database backups, and bastion-host dumps were in publicly listable cloud storage. No customer notification. No apology. No scope statement.

LDPlayer Software UNCHANGED

Most recent release: 9.5.11.1, dated May 6 — seven days before disclosure. Twelve days after disclosure, no new release. The LDAppStore binary inside the current release is byte-for-byte identical (same SHA-256) to the March release. The defensive update a security-conscious vendor would push has not arrived.

Kuro Games DEFLECTED

Only game company that responded. Issued May 15 via a Wuthering Waves player ticket, treating the issue as a third-party-recharge customer service complaint rather than a credential exposure incident. Risk put on the user.

HoYoverse / Yostar / Hypergryph SILENT

No public response. No internal acknowledgement. HoYoverse received the disclosure at their published privacy contact; Yostar at both account-contact webform and Arknights support. Both addresses accepted delivery. Neither has produced a follow-up.

Huawei Cloud ACTED

No reply from the cloud abuse address, but the buckets were locked down by 8:02 AM May 14 — roughly twenty-four hours after Part 1 went out. All twenty-three previously-listable buckets now return 403 Access Denied to anonymous listing. The cleanest reading is that Huawei acted on the abuse report.

Summary 1 of 4

One of four game publishers responded, and that response was a deflection. Three publishers said nothing. The cloud provider acted but did not communicate. The operator has been completely silent in public and has shipped no defensive update to their software in nineteen days.

What You Should Do — Tonight

If you have ever used LDPlayer, LD Cloud, LDShop, EasyFun, FunPass, FunPG, LDYunOS, or anything routed through oslink.io:

  1. Rotate every game and account password you have entered while using any of these. Specifically the ones named in the original disclosure: Genshin Impact, Wuthering Waves, Neverness to Everness, Arknights and Arknights: Endfield, Honkai: Star Rail, Zenless Zone Zero, plus any payment or storefront login.
  2. Turn on two-factor authentication on those accounts. Prefer an authenticator app or hardware security key over SMS. SMS- based 2FA is better than nothing but it is the weakest option.
  3. Review login history and active sessions in each game's account portal. Revoke any session you do not recognise.
  4. If you reused those passwords anywhere else, rotate them there too.
  5. Stop using LDPlayer until the operator publicly explains what they collect, why, and demonstrates that the collection has been corrected. The cost of switching to a different Android emulator for a few weeks is one evening. The cost of staying on a product whose operator will not acknowledge a documented credential exposure is unbounded.
  6. Watch your card and bank statements over the next several weeks if you ran any subscription, top-up, or in-game purchase through any of these.
  7. Sign up for free notifications at haveibeenpwned.com/NotifyMe with the email address you used in any LD product. When the leaked databases get formally ingested by HIBP, you will get an automated email and can check whether you were in the affected set without ever needing to touch the raw data yourself.

Why the Advice Didn't Change but the Reasoning Did

The IME-keylogger pathway I described in Part 1 was a hypothesis, and it only applies to users who actively installed Sogou through LDAppStore.

The bigger and broader risk, for everyone with LDPlayer installed (including users who never went near Sogou), is that the bundled LDAppStore ships device telemetry to a third-party SDK that an independent research lab has documented as having broken encryption and unauthenticated TLS.

That risk has been present for months. The current public LDPlayer release still ships it unchanged.

The Broader Pattern

LDPlayer is one of the most widely used Android emulators in the gacha community. Anyone who plays Chinese gacha titles on PC, anyone who uses an emulator to multi-account, anyone who switched to an emulator because their phone could not handle the game, has touched this product. The user base is much larger than the cloud streaming service that was the subject of Part 1.

LD's response over the past twelve days:

  • Silence on their own channels
  • No defensive software update
  • No customer notification

Kuro's response: tell affected players the risk is on them.

The other three game publishers: nothing.

// The Pattern

The bucket exposure could be a single misconfiguration. The third- party SDK with broken encryption is upstream of LD, not LD's fault directly. The unpatched Android version is an industry-wide problem with emulator vendors. Each of those, individually, has a non- malicious explanation. The pattern that holds them together — a vendor who handles credential-adjacent data at scale and consistently treats user safety as a lower priority than shipping features — is what should drive your decision about whether to keep this product installed.
